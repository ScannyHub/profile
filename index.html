Automating Remotely Sensed Shoreline Detection With Cloud Computation

Background
In brief:
Googles' Cloud service was used for the computation of detecting and outlining shorelines in hundreds of satellite photos with no human input, illustrating previously intensive workloads for desktop computers can now be accomplished on any device with minimal analyst input.

That got me get a first-class honours. You can view the code on Github or on Google Earth Engine.

The work itself is grounded in relevance, knowledge of shoreline positions is a critical need spanning a range of applications from coastal science to property management. While cloud computation and storage is not new, nor automated shoreline detection, the combination of these factors within Google Earth Engine (GEE) is. Provided was an algorithm for automated processing of satellite imagery, creating annual mean shorelines over any given period.


Challenge

  • No literature on the subject involving GEE.
  • Large datasets, and applicability outside of study area.
  • Minimal analyst input required, algorithm must be automated.

Solution

  • Extensive research, and trialing of new ideas.
  • Target shared attributes, automated classifcation.
  • Use of automatic cluster-based thresholds (Otsu).

Synthesis

My ability to deliver in-depth, self-directed results at a high standard under pressure has been rigoursly benchmarked.

Key Aspects and Tools:
  • Google Earth Engine, Landsat series, Javascript.
  • Harnessing big data, in the form of freely-available satellite imagery.
  • Ability to synthesise and communicate diverse amount of information.
  • Rapid understanding of cutting edge technologies for delivering practical results.

Browser Based Analytics

Background

During my time in the Geospatial Services team of New Zealand’s largest water and wastewater provider, Watercare, I had the opportunity to provide a series user-friendly visualisations of ArcGIS software analytics.


The scenario: Watercare has a limited amount of ArcGIS licences that are issued upon user request; requests are accepted or declined depending on the users permissions. Managment sought to track who had a licence token active, have an analysis of usage over any given time, and a log of who is denied access.


I undertook this challenge by harnessing Python for file processing, heavily leveraging the Pandas library for statistical analysis and data manipulation, and HTML/JS/CSS with Plotly (JS) for visualisation. The finalised web application provides dynamic tracking of software statistics and allows instantaneous information on who has access to what licences, invaluable when requesting access to limited extensions.


Challenge

  • Convert poorly constructed logs into processible files.
  • Convert processed data into files of descriptive statistics.
  • Provide a simple, clean, web interface for interactive analysis.

Solution

  • Python for a quickly built processing script.
  • Expand scope of script to statistical via Pandas.
  • Craft a minimalist interface via Plotly for Javascript.

Synthesis

My computational knowledge allows me to provide elegant solutions to issues stretching from data-processing to UX/UI.

Key Aspects and Tools:
  • Python and the Pandas library for statistical analysis.
  • Understanding of how to create a simple, intuative user interface.
  • Javascript/CSS/HTML and the Plotly (D3) library for web visualisation.
  • Testing and integration of a web application across a range of server-based environments.

Snakes, Tapes, and Penguins Galore

This multifaceted challenge arose during my time in the Data Services team of Land Information New Zealand, the national geographic & survey institution.


A significant amount of historical data existed solely within a box of magnetic tapes. Due to threats of deterioration over time, and a series of major earthquakes, it was deemed imperative to convert the data to digital. Restoration turned out to be a complex workflow, procuring older hardware parts to enable comparability with the magnetic tape reader. For the tapes to be read on modern machines, the legacy software had to be located and installed, a pair of virtual machines running Ubuntu setup, and a series of python and shell scripts for the data processing were created.

Each problem solved spawned a new set, but success was ensured by recording each step involved and iteratively testing and documenting different steps taken and methodologies used.

Ground Control, a UAV, and Me


Surveying with RTK-GPS, setting base-stations, determining ground control points for UAV survey, long days in the field, and dealing with problems on the ground are facts of life I have been thoroughly acquainted with.

The culmination of these hurdles occurred during my time performing research in the Tarndale Valley in Gisborne, New Zealand. However, having the validation of presenting my findings at a conference of Australasian geological experts more than made up for the trouble.

The goal of the research was to test the validity of contemporary landform evolution models through numerical modelling, and to trial what was a novel methodology at the time, the use of a UAV to quickly and easily collect high-quality digital elevation models.

The synthesis of the work was that the theoretical landform evolution models were confirmed as valid, demonstrated was the practicality of using a UAV to collect DEM data for the rapid acquisition of high-quality topographic models.

While flying the drone was easy enough, trekking across a gully only accessible by foot, and teetering along the edge of the scarp to establish ground control points took a significant amount of time and effort! An in-depth explanation can be found on page 38 of the ANZGG conference schedule.
